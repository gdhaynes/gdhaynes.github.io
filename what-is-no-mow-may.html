What is No Mow May

No Mow May and its cousin Low Mow May are a movement to avoid or reduce lawn mowing during mid and late spring which often occurs during May in higher latitudes, but may be in April or even March in lower latitudes. The reason for this is that at the same time our lawns are greening up and in need of a mow, flowers in our lawn are beginning to bloom. These lawn flowers have been shown to provide a food source to bees, whose numbers are in decline, when other flowers are scarce. Conventional mowing as well as herbicide application would remove these important food sources from the food chain.

Tips For No Mow May

No Mow May is new on the scene, here are some tips to help if you choose to participate:

  1. Educate your Neighbors

    After a few weeks of not mowing, your lawn is going to look somewhat shabby and your neighbors may begin to worry or may be not too keen on your long shaggy grass. Xerces Society has free print at home signs you can print out and display in your window.

  2. Check Your Local Rules

    Local municipalities and homeowners associations (HOAs) will have rules about how long your grass can be. If there is a rule you may need to instead participate in Low Mow May, where instead of not mowing at all all you simply mow less and at a greater height.

  3. Avoid herbicides and pesticides

    Herbicides and pesticides defeat the purpose, avoid using these products in general, but if you must use them avoid using them until later in the summer.

  4. Engage with Community leaders

    Contact your city officials or HOA board members and ask them to adopt No Mow May in your community. Communities that adopt No Mow May will suspend enforcement of grass height rules for participating residents. And even if your community doesn't have grass height rules, an official endorsement may make other residents more comfortable with participating.

  5. Cut back your lawn gradually

    At the end of May, or whenever your no mow period is, gradually cut your lawn back down to its normal height. Do this by gradually reducing the height over a series of mows.

  6. Consider a Pollinator Garden

    Want to participate in No Mow May during the entire growing season? Or maybe your municipality or HOA won't allow you to participate in No Mow May? Consider planting a pollinator garden, these are gardens filled with a variety of native flowers so that there is almost always a flower in bloom. These not only help the bees, but also look great in any yard.
